01What this dossier is, and how to read it

This is an equal-weight overview of the main avenues to quantum computing, as they stand in mid-2026. It is not advocacy for one platform. It is a map of where each approach actually is, what it has demonstrated, and an honest — deliberately loose — estimate of each one's odds of going all the way. The weighting across avenues is equal for now; that will change as the research lands.

One distinction governs everything below, because it is the single most common way this topic gets misreported: the difference between a and a . A raw qubit count is not an error-corrected qubit count, and the two can differ by hundreds to one. We state which is which everywhere.

By success we mean one specific thing: an avenue reaching quantum computing on a roughly 10–15 year horizon. An avenue that produces a striking demo but never reaches that finish line does not count as success under this definition.

How to read the probabilities. Each avenue below carries a percentage. These are independent, loose per-path odds — each answers "will this path reach the finish line?" on its own. They are not a pie: they do not, and should not, sum to 100, because more than one avenue can succeed (or all can fail). Each figure is soft (±~15 points), a mid-2026 snapshot, and an OPEN-UNVERIFIED author forecast this dossier fully expects to revise as evidence accumulates. Every one is posted as an open challenge in the claim ledger (ids QCF1–QCF8).

Citation audit pending. The sources cited below are shown as reported by the cited work. The Phase-4 citation audit — re-fetching and checking each one against primary sources — has not yet been done; it will appear in the audit trail. Until then, treat every source chip as "reported, not yet independently verified." No source here is marked verified.

02The avenues

Eight gate-model avenues, plus an annealing aside that answers a different question. For each: the demonstrated basis, the case for and against, then the forecast box. Where a date or milestone comes from a company's own roadmap rather than an independent result, it is attributed to the vendor and labeled as a projection.

Superconducting (Google, IBM, Rigetti, IQM)

Basis. Google's Willow processor (105 physical qubits) demonstrated — the logical error rate fell as the code grew (distance 3→5→7), reaching about 0.14% logical error per cycle with real-time decoding . IBM's Condor reached 1,121 physical qubits before IBM pivoted to smaller, higher-quality modular chips .

Pros. Most mature at scale; fast gates; the most capital and the deepest bench of serious players, which de-risks the path. Cons. The overhead is heavy (hundreds-to-thousands of physical qubits per logical qubit), everything runs cryogenic, and chip-to-chip interconnect is the open scaling frontier. IBM's published roadmap targets large-scale fault tolerance around 2029 — the company's own projection, not an independent result.

OPEN-UNVERIFIEDForecast — Superconducting: ~70%. Most mature, most capital, fastest gates, and error correction already improving with scale; multiple serious players de-risk it. Signpost: revises up if modular interconnect and logical scaling keep working; down if the overhead proves intractable. Loose, independent per-path odds (±~15 pts, mid-2026); ledger id QCF1 — open challenge, named credit to whoever sharpens it.

Trapped ion (Quantinuum, IonQ incl. Oxford Ionics)

Basis. Quantinuum's Helios ran 98 physical barium ions at ~99.92% two-qubit fidelity across all pairs, yielding 48 error-corrected logical qubits at roughly a 2:1 . Oxford Ionics / IonQ have reported the highest gate fidelities of any platform (two-qubit ~99.99%) .

Pros. Highest demonstrated quality — accuracy and coherence — and the best (lowest) encoding ratio on the logical-qubit leaderboard. Cons. Slower gates, and laser/optics complexity has been the historic scaling bottleneck; pushing qubit counts high while keeping fidelity intact is the open question.

OPEN-UNVERIFIEDForecast — Trapped ion: ~60%. Highest demonstrated quality and encoding efficiency; the open question is scaling qubit count, not quality. Signpost: revises up if a chip-based ion system scales past hundreds of qubits with fidelity intact. Loose, independent odds (±~15 pts, mid-2026); ledger id QCF2.

Neutral atom (QuEra, Pasqal, Atom Computing)

Basis. QuEra reported 96 logical qubits from 448 physical atoms with below-threshold error suppression — the current logical-qubit count leader . Atom Computing crossed 1,000 physical atoms, and Pasqal has run ~1,000-atom systems .

Pros. Fastest recent progress; leads in error-corrected qubit count; atoms are identical by physics (no device-to-device variation), and connectivity is reconfigurable in software rather than fixed by lithography. Cons. A younger control/gate stack; gate speed and fidelity are still maturing relative to ions.

OPEN-UNVERIFIEDForecast — Neutral atom: ~58%. Fastest mover, leads on error-corrected qubit count, cheap identical atoms, software-defined connectivity; held back mainly by relative youth. Signpost: revises up with sustained large logical-qubit operation at higher fidelity. Loose, independent odds (±~15 pts, mid-2026); ledger id QCF3.

Silicon spin (Intel, Diraq, Silicon Quantum Computing, Quantum Motion, Equal1)

Basis. Diraq with imec showed >99% two-qubit fidelity for silicon spin qubits on a standard 300 mm CMOS foundry line — and across multiple randomly chosen dies, not one hero device . Silicon Quantum Computing reported ~99.99% two-qubit fidelity . These silicon spin qubits are quantum-dot qubits — the electron's spin is held in an electrostatically-defined quantum dot in the semiconductor — so this avenue is the "quantum-dot computing" approach under its more specific name.

Pros. The best manufacturing and scaling story of any avenue — it rides the existing semiconductor industry, in principle the cheapest path to millions of qubits — and it has now hit top-tier quality on an industrial line. Cons. The fewest qubits so far, and no logical-qubit demonstration yet on this modality. High variance.

OPEN-UNVERIFIEDForecast — Silicon spin: ~45%. Best manufacturing/scaling argument and freshly proven quality on a real foundry line, but the furthest to go on logical qubits — so the spread is wide. Signpost: revises up sharply with a first credible logical qubit built on silicon spin. Loose, independent odds (±~15 pts, mid-2026); ledger id QCF4.

Photonic (PsiQuantum, Xanadu, Quandela)

Basis. Xanadu's Aurora — a 12-qubit modular, networked, room-temperature photonic machine built from 35 chips and ~13 km of fiber — plus on-chip generation of error-resistant GKP states .

Pros. Room-temperature operation, and networking and modularity are native rather than bolted on; it rides silicon-photonics fabrication. Cons. is the make-or-break bottleneck and is not yet solved, and many gates are inherently probabilistic, demanding heavy redundancy.

OPEN-UNVERIFIEDForecast — Photonic: ~35%. Real, distinctive advantages (room temperature, native networking), but gated almost entirely by unsolved optical loss and probabilistic gates. Signpost: revises up materially if optical loss drops below the fault-tolerance threshold with a logical qubit demonstrated. Loose, independent odds (±~15 pts, mid-2026); ledger id QCF5. See §03 — the author's thesis bets on exactly this.

III-V quantum dots (optically-active / photon sources)

Basis. Dots made from compound (III-V) semiconductors — typically indium arsenide or gallium arsenide — that emit single photons on demand and can host a spin that interfaces with light; Quandela is a representative builder. (Distinct from the silicon-spin dots above: those trap a spin to compute in the chip; these are optical, emitting photons that feed photonic and network architectures.)

Pros. Among the best on-demand single-photon sources available — high purity and indistinguishability — plus a natural spin-photon interface, which makes them strong as inputs to photonic computing and as nodes for quantum networking. Cons. Dot-to-dot variability (each grown dot differs slightly), and they function more as a component feeding photonic/network architectures than as a self-contained general processor.

OPEN-UNVERIFIEDForecast — III-V quantum dots: ~10–12%. The physics is strong, but as a route to a general fault-tolerant computer it is weak — dot-to-dot variability, and they act as a component feeding photonic/network architectures rather than a self-contained processor. As with NV, this ~10–12% measures the fault-tolerant, useful-scale computing question specifically; it is not a judgement that the technology is weak — its real strength is as a best-in-class photon source and spin-photon network interface, a different lane this number does not measure. Signpost: revises up with a credible scalable processor architecture built on III-V quantum dots — not merely an improved photon-source benchmark. Loose, independent odds (±~15 pts, mid-2026); ledger id QCF10.

Topological (Microsoft)

Basis — and the dispute. Microsoft presented Majorana 1 as 8 topological qubits and a hardware path toward a million-qubit chip . This claim is contested: the accompanying peer reviewers noted the results do not by themselves constitute evidence for the underlying Majorana zero modes, and much of the physics community remains unconvinced that the device demonstrates a topological qubit at all . A follow-up reports longer lifetimes but leaves the central physics question open.

Pros. If real, intrinsic hardware-level error protection — a potentially decisive scaling advantage, which is exactly why it is worth pursuing and exactly why the bar for "demonstrated" must stay high. Cons. The existence of the qubit itself is not yet accepted; this is a materials-science bet whose foundational claim is disputed.

OPEN-UNVERIFIEDForecast — Topological: ~15%. A bet on an unproven physics claim; low expected value precisely because the existence question is still open. Signpost: independent reproduction of an accepted topological qubit — which could collapse this toward 0 or jump it materially. Loose, independent odds (±~15 pts, mid-2026); ledger id QCF6.

NV centers (nitrogen-vacancy in diamond)

Basis. The qubit is a defect in a diamond crystal — a nitrogen atom sitting beside a missing carbon atom — and the computation uses the spin of an electron trapped at that site.

Pros. Operates at room temperature, with no dilution refrigerator; long spin coherence; and a natural optical interface (the spin can be read out and controlled with light), which makes NV centres outstanding for quantum networking, sensing, and communication. Cons. It has proven very hard to scale into large, connected processors: accurately placing and wiring many NV qubits in diamond is the unsolved bottleneck, and the field has largely converged on NV being superb for sensors and network nodes rather than a route to a general fault-tolerant computer.

OPEN-UNVERIFIEDForecast — NV centers: ~12%. The physics is strong, but the case for scalable, general-purpose computing is weak — qubit placement and wiring in diamond is the unsolved problem. This ~12% measures the fault-tolerant, useful-scale computing question specifically; it is not a judgement that NV is weak — its real strength points at sensing and networking, a different lane this number does not measure. Signpost: revises up with a credible multi-qubit error-corrected NV processor architecture that has a real scaling path — not another sensing or networking milestone. Loose, independent odds (±~15 pts, mid-2026); ledger id QCF9.

Annealing / D-Wave — an aside, not ranked head-to-head

D-Wave's annealers are in commercial optimization use today — a real, already-banked win — but is not universal gate-based computing, so it sits outside the fault-tolerance race this dossier scores. D-Wave has separately announced a gate-model roadmap (a small initial system, targeting 100 logical qubits by 2032 — the company's projection) .

OPEN-UNVERIFIEDForecast — D-Wave gate-model pivot: ~30% (aside, not ranked against the eight above). The present-day annealing business is a separate, already-banked success answering a different question. Signpost: revises up if the gate-model line delivers logical qubits on its roadmap. Loose (mid-2026); ledger id QCF7.

03The optical-versus-electronic question

There is a longer-range question underneath the avenue-by-avenue scoring: what is the eventual substrate of a mature quantum computer? The author's thesis — held with conviction after years of following this field — is that the long-term endpoint is optical/photonic rather than electronic: room-temperature operation and native networking are the properties a planetary-scale, interconnected quantum infrastructure will ultimately demand.

Honesty requires flagging the tension directly. This thesis runs ahead of the demonstrated evidence. Today the evidence ranks photonic lower than the electronic avenues (~35%, §02), precisely because optical loss is unsolved. Conviction and demonstration are different columns of this ledger: the author's belief informs which question to chase; it does not get to set the photonic probability. The number moves when the evidence moves — not before.

OPEN-UNVERIFIEDAuthor thesis (tracked forecast): the long-term endpoint of quantum computing is optical/photonic rather than electronic. Stated plainly as the author's conviction, currently ahead of the demonstrated evidence. Signpost: if photonic optical loss drops below the fault-tolerance threshold with a working logical qubit, the photonic probability (QCF5) revises up materially and this thesis gains ground. Ledger id QCF8 — an open challenge: confirm, refute, or sharpen it, with named credit in the next version.

No avenue has won. The job of this dossier is the honest separation of what has been demonstrated from what is being predicted — and to move the odds only when the evidence does.

04$2 Billion, Two Decades Too Late

In May 2026 the United States committed about $2 billion to quantum technology under the CHIPS and Science Act — a real step, and a recent one. The instinct is to set it beside a headline Chinese number and call the race. This section argues the opposite: the dollar-to-dollar comparison is the wrong lens. It makes the case instead on the axes that actually compare — institutional tempo, structural commitment, and what the spending buys — and closes with a thesis in the author's own voice.

Hefei: "Quantum Avenue" and the national lab

Sourced. China's effort is anchored in Hefei, home to the National Laboratory for Quantum Information Sciences, set in motion after Xi Jinping's 2016 visit to USTC and described by some outlets as the world's largest quantum facility . Along Yunfei Road, more than thirty quantum companies cluster on what locals call — a real dedicated district, though a cluster on an avenue rather than a purpose-built "quantum city." The one verified construction figure is modest — MERICS confirms roughly CNY 7 billion (~$1B) for Phase 1 — and the section on dollars below explains why even that is the wrong thing to fixate on.

Pan Jianwei and the USTC milestones

Sourced. Most of China's headline results trace to Pan Jianwei's group at USTC: the Micius satellite's first space-to-ground quantum key distribution and the ~2,032 km Beijing–Shanghai QKD backbone ; the Jiuzhang photonic boson-sampling "quantum advantage," with Jiuzhang 3.0 reportedly reaching 255 photons ; and the Zuchongzhi superconducting processors . Note the shape of this lead: it is strongest in quantum communication and in sampling-style advantage demos — neither of which is a fault-tolerant, useful-scale computer of the kind §02 scores.

Private pullback, state effort still rising

Sourced — and this cuts against the simple story. China's consumer-tech champions pulled back: Alibaba closed its quantum lab in November 2023 and Baidu in January 2024, donating their equipment to universities and pivoting to AI . But the overall Chinese effort did not shrink — it grew. State-aligned specialists such as Origin Quantum and QuantumCTek took the commercial baton , and the funding trajectory turned sharply up: the 15th Five-Year Plan (2026) names quantum an industrial priority, a national guidance fund earmarked ~CNY 121.8B across three regional vehicles, and first-quarter-2026 financing alone (~CNY 2.2B) nearly matched all of 2025 . The honest reading is not "China is retreating" but "the baton passed from big tech to the state, and the spending kept rising."

The universities and the talent pipeline

Sourced. A quantum effort is ultimately people, and China has built the pipeline to supply them. The initiative (双一流, launched 2015; lists published 2017) designated 42 "world-class" universities and 465 first-class disciplines across 140 institutions, aiming to become a "higher-education powerhouse" by 2050; the Ministry of Education then named twelve universities to "Schools of Future Technology," tasked explicitly with rivalling MIT and Stanford in science and technology . Running alongside is an aggressive, internationally-advertised recruitment drive — the Thousand Talents Plan and its successors court STEM researchers abroad with large multi-year packages, a campaign still active today, as when a 2024 program reportedly approached 149 professors (about a fifth of the faculty) at a single Korean institute . The institutions and the recruitment are two halves of one durable, decades-scale bet on STEM capacity.

Why the dollar comparison is the wrong lens

Sourced. Reaching for a single Chinese spending figure is a trap twice over. First, the headline numbers are unreliable: the most-quoted "~$15B" traces to one 2022 McKinsey estimate whose own caveats flag thin China data, with same-lineage figures running as high as $150B and an insider putting the real total at perhaps a third — this dossier rests on none of them. Second, and more important, even an accurate dollar figure would mislead, because a research dollar buys far more in China: lower labour, materials, fabrication and construction costs mean each unit of spend yields more people and more hardware. For every $100,000 of R&D, Chinese firms field roughly 2.3 researchers — more than double the US figure — and on a purchasing-power basis China has drawn level with or passed the US in total R&D (the OECD puts 2024 at about $1.03T vs $1.01T, with cost-adjusted estimates wider still) . Dollar-to-dollar understates effective Chinese output, so the dollar axis is the wrong one to argue on. The axes that do compare are tempo, structural commitment, and what the money buys.

And is the US moving fast enough?

Sourced. On tempo, the contrast is stark. The US National Quantum Initiative — the 2018 law that organized the federal effort — saw its core R&D authorization lapse on 30 September 2023, and reauthorization stalled in committee through 2024, only re-advancing in 2026 . Meanwhile a federal advisory panel has urged Congress to adopt a "Quantum First" national goal by 2030, and industry voices (Microsoft among them) warned the US risks falling behind and pressed for a bigger STEM-talent pipeline . The $2B is real — but it lands after the flagship program had been allowed to lapse for over two years, against a Chinese effort organized in standing five-year plans.

The thesis — the author's wake-up call

Here I step out of the reporter's chair. My view, after years of following this field, is not that the West has already lost — it is a question I think we are not asking loudly enough: are we doing enough, and early enough? China set a multi-decade course in the late 2000s, stood up the world's most-publicized quantum lab by 2017, kept spending through its own big-tech pullback, and has been building the universities and recruiting the people to staff the effort for a generation. The US, by contrast, let its flagship initiative lapse and is still re-assembling the response. That gap in tempo and persistence — not any dollar figure — is what should worry us.

Honesty requires the counter-evidence in the same breath: the dollar figures everyone quotes are unreliable in either direction; much of China's lead is in quantum communication, where the US largely chose not to compete; and most independent assessments still put the US ahead in quantum computing — the very lane §02 scores. So this is a wake-up call, not an obituary.

OPEN-UNVERIFIEDAuthor argument (the author's view): the question is not whether the West has already lost, but whether US/allied quantum investment, institutions and talent pipelines are large enough and early enough to keep pace with a two-decade Chinese program that is still accelerating — judged on tempo and structure, not on unreliable dollar totals. A provocative argument in the author's own voice, not a settled finding, and held in tension by the counter-evidence above. Falsifiable signpost: the worry is eased if the US/allies sustain a reauthorized, fully-funded national quantum program and are first to a fault-tolerant, useful-scale machine; it is sharpened if China is first to fault-tolerant useful-scale computing — or fields an operational, unbreakable continental quantum network — while Western funding stays stop-start. Ledger id CN6.

The lab is real, the milestones are real, and the dollar totals are a guess in every direction — the question that should keep us up is not who is spending more, but who is building, and staffing, without pause.

05An independent AI judgment on §04 — an experiment

What this section is — and is not. This is an experiment: an independent assessment of the previous section's thesis written by the AI model that helped produce this dossier (Opus 4.8). It is offered as one additional data point for you to weigh — not an authority, and emphatically not a "the AI said it, so it's true" verdict. For the experiment to mean anything, the model was handed the evidence below and the actual text of §04 and asked to reach its own conclusion; it was not told what to decide, and was free to disagree with the author. Read it as you would a sharp outside referee — useful, fallible, and yours to overrule.

The claim under review is §04's thesis (ledger CN6): not "the West has already lost," but the narrower, hedged worry — are we doing enough, and early enough? — argued from tempo and structure, and explicitly conceding that most assessments still put the US ahead in quantum computing. The question here is whether that thesis is overstated, understated, or fairly stated. Three bodies of evidence bear on it.

The macro base rate: the Chinese S-curve

Start with the outside view. Fifteen-to-twenty years ago China was a non-factor in electric vehicles and batteries; today Chinese firms install roughly 70–75% of the world's EV batteries, with CATL (~38% globally) and BYD (~17%) leading, LFP chemistry making up over 80% of domestic installs, and China commercializing sodium-ion cells while the field still chases solid-state . The lesson the world keeps re-learning is blunt: do not bet against the Chinese S-curve on a two-decade horizon. That base rate is real, and it is a legitimate reason to take a "wake-up call" seriously rather than wave it away.

Does that base rate transfer to quantum?

Here is the discipline the outside view needs. The EV and battery triumph is, at its core, a manufacturing, supply-chain and incremental-chemistry story — precisely China's demonstrated strength. Fault-tolerant quantum computing is not purely that. Several of its hardest gates are fundamental-science unknowns where industrial muscle does not obviously help: whether a topological qubit even exists (§02, ~15%), or whether optical loss can be driven below the fault-tolerance threshold (§02, photonic ~35%). So the base rate transfers unevenly — it bites hard on the parts of QC that are now scale-up problems (fabrication, qubit count, control systems, cryogenics) and weakly on the parts still gated by physics. And tellingly, the strongest near-term fault-tolerance results to date — below-threshold error correction, record gate fidelities, the largest logical-qubit counts — are presently Western/US-led (§02). The author's concession on that point is correct and load-bearing.

But is China weak in fundamental science? The publication record

The reflex objection — "China industrializes, it does not do basic science" — does not survive the data. China is now the largest producer of scientific papers overall (passing the US in 2024 for the first time since the US passed the UK in 1948) and has led the world's top-1% most-cited papers since around 2019 (27.2% vs the US 24.9% for 2018–2020) . In high-impact natural science it tops the Nature Index, with its advantage concentrated precisely in physics and chemistry — and the Chinese Academy of Sciences, USTC (Pan Jianwei's institution) and Tsinghua among the top institutions worldwide . The honest caveats belong here too: China's high rate of self-citation inflates some rankings, paper-mill and fraud problems are real and increasingly exposed, and by some quality-weighted measures the US still leads . But "weak in fundamental physics" is no longer a tenable description of the country whose flagship institute produced Micius and Jiuzhang.

The verdict

Weighing all three: §04's thesis is fairly stated in form, and if anything modestly understated in force. In form it is well-calibrated — it refuses to call the race, frames itself as a question, and concedes the current US lead in computing; that restraint is correct, because the science-gated parts of QC genuinely cap how far a manufacturing base rate can carry China. But in force the author left value on the table: the single most powerful support for the "wake-up call" — that China now leads high-impact physics output and hosts top-three physics institutions — goes unmentioned in §04, which leans instead on funding, tempo and talent. The base rate and the publication record together make the alarm more warranted than the section's careful hedging conveys. Where I would resist any stronger version of the thesis is exactly where the author already stopped: a clean prediction that "China wins fault-tolerant QC" is not supported, because the decisive near-term results are Western and the remaining gates are partly basic-physics, not industrial.

06What the US should do — a strategic argument

What this section is. Everything below is a strategic argument — reasoned policy recommendations, not verified findings. The factual anchors (what programs exist, real funding figures, documented track records) are cited; the prescriptions built on them are reasoning, and belong in the OPEN column. Treat them as one informed view among many, there to be argued with — each recommendation is labelled RECOMMENDATION, and the closing counter-view is labelled as such.

§05 surfaced an uncomfortable fact: the gap is not only in quantum but upstream — in basic-science output and in the industrial S-curve that turns science into product, where China now leads. If §04's worry is real, the answer cannot be a quantum-only patch. This section sketches what regaining and holding macro leadership would take, across three pillars — scientific research, industrialisation, and the quantum question nested inside both — then weighs the options for dealing with China's industrial capacity, and closes with the honest counter-case that dominance may be the wrong goal altogether.

Pillar 1 — Regain scientific-research leadership

Cited context. The irony is that the US already legislated the fix and then declined to fund it. The CHIPS and Science Act authorised a historic science surge, but appropriations fell about 42% short for the NSF in FY24 ($9.06B against $15.64B authorised), the combined NSF/NIST/DOE-Science gap exceeded $8B, and the new NSF technology directorate received roughly $410M of about $20B authorised . Meanwhile China leads the high-impact Nature Index in physics and chemistry and the top-1% most-cited papers , and the National Quantum Initiative's core authorisation was allowed to lapse for over two years . Proposals like the Endless Frontier Act ($100B over five years, a new NSF tech directorate) show the ambition exists; the bottleneck is appropriation and consistency .

RECOMMENDATIONFund the science surge that is already on the books — stably. The single highest-leverage move is also the least glamorous: appropriate the basic-research funding CHIPS already authorised, and do it multi-year, because basic research compounds and stop-start funding is its enemy; reauthorise and fully fund the NQI; protect the NSF's basic-science core even as the applied tech directorate grows. Trade-offs: real budget cost; the political difficulty of sustained appropriations; and the risk (raised by Endless-Frontier critics) of securitising and distorting basic science toward short-term competition. Policy argument, ledger id US4.

Pillar 2 — Build a competitive industrialisation S-curve

Cited context. Science leadership without an industrial flywheel just yields papers other countries commercialise. China's EV and battery rise is the template the US has to answer: relentless scale-up, cost-down, and chemistry iteration. US reshoring has had real but mixed results — over $630B committed across ~140 projects and ~500k jobs since 2020, but US fab costs run 30–50% above Asia, skilled labour is short, and flagship projects such as TSMC Arizona slipped years . With a factory dollar (like a research dollar) buying more in China , brute-force reshoring alone will not close the gap.

RECOMMENDATIONBuild the flywheel deliberately, not just the fabs. Pair supply-side investment with durable demand-side pull (procurement, deployment incentives) so domestic industries can ride their own learning curve rather than stand up idle capacity; concentrate on sectors where scale compounds (batteries, advanced packaging, quantum-enabling fabrication); and treat the workforce as the binding constraint, not an afterthought. Trade-offs: cost, time, and the genuine risk of subsidising industries that never reach competitiveness. Policy argument, ledger id US5.

Exploiting — and competing with — China's industrial capacity: an options menu

The choice is not one lever but a portfolio, and each option carries a cost. Friend-shoring / allied supply chains — build redundancy with allies (the nascent US-India fabs, plus Japan, Korea, the EU): cheaper than full reshoring, but slower and diplomatically complex. Buy-and-improve / leverage interdependence — use China's cheap, mature hardware where it is not security-critical and concentrate US effort on the higher-value innovation layered on top: fast and cost-effective, but it deepens dependence in those layers. Reshoring incentives, tariffs and industrial policy — rebuild domestic capacity, but against a 30–50% cost premium and a mixed track record, while tariffs raise input costs and invite retaliation. Talent and immigration — the highest-leverage, lowest-cost lever of all: capture the global talent pool (including the very researchers China courts), politically contested as it is . Public-private fabrication and standards/IP leadership — own the high-margin layers and write the rules. Export controls and selective decoupling — restrict the narrow set of QC-enabling tooling to slow a rival, accepting the trade-off that controls accelerate indigenous substitution and fragment markets; a tactical brake, not a strategy.

RECOMMENDATIONRun a managed portfolio, not a single ideology. Match each tool to a sector's security-criticality; lead with the cheapest, highest-leverage moves (talent, stable science funding, allied supply chains) and reserve coercive instruments (broad tariffs, decoupling, export controls) for the narrow cases where security genuinely outweighs their cost. This is deliberately options analysis — weighing legitimate competitive strategies and their trade-offs — not a playbook for covertly sabotaging another economy. Policy argument, ledger id US6.

Pillar 3 — The quantum question, nested in the macro one

QC leadership is necessary but not sufficient — and pursued in isolation it may be the wrong target. A country that leads in quantum yet trails in the broad science-and-industry base will not hold that lead, because the next decisive technology will emerge from the base, not the moonshot. The §02 evidence is encouraging here: the US still leads the near-term fault-tolerance race. The task is therefore to convert that lead into durable advantage by strengthening the macro foundation beneath it — not to treat quantum as a standalone sprint.

RECOMMENDATIONFund QC as one node in a coherent science-and-industry strategy. Protect the present US fault-tolerance lead (a sustained NQI, fabrication access, talent) while building the macro base any future lead depends on; avoid over-indexing on a single modality or a single technology. The quantum win, if it comes, will be a by-product of getting the fundamentals right. Policy argument, ledger id US7.

Counter-view — is dominance even the right goal?

This whole section has assumed "leadership," even "dominance," is the objective. The honest counter-case deserves equal air. Much frontier science is inherently international: the Standard Model, gravitational-wave detection, the human genome and mRNA vaccines all came from cross-border collaboration, and quantum theory itself is globally shared — a dominance frame that securitises science can deter the very collaboration and talent inflows that produce breakthroughs, with the recent visa own-goals a live example . Resilience may also matter more than supremacy: a system that can absorb shocks and stay at the frontier may be wiser, and cheaper, than one straining to be number one in everything. And zero-sum framing can be self-fulfilling — an arms-race dynamic that burns resources and forecloses positive-sum gains.

COUNTER-VIEWMaybe the goal should be frontier-presence and resilience, not dominance. A defensible alternative: stay at the scientific frontier, build resilient (not autarkic) supply, lead through openness and standards, and compete hard only where security genuinely demands it — rather than pursue dominance as an end in itself. Offered to keep this section strategy rather than manifesto; weigh it against the pillars above. Policy argument / counter-argument, ledger id US8.

Leadership is downstream of two choices the US can still make for itself: whether it funds its own science like it means it, and whether it lets the world's best minds come and build here. The quantum race is just the part of that choice visible at the moment.

07The future is not simply more of the past

What this section is. This closing section is forward-looking and partly speculative. Documented current capabilities are cited; claims about the future are labelled FORWARD VIEW — argument and forecast, not fact. No confident dates the evidence cannot support, and no breathless futurism: where a direction is argued, its limits and its counter-case are argued alongside it.

§06 argued for funding science and industry well. But "fund the old institutions better" quietly assumes the future is just more of the past — more money into the same labs and the same fabs. The deeper question for a country trying to lead is whether the form of research and industry is about to change, and whether to lay new tracks rather than only widen the old ones. Below: what AI and robotics demonstrably do today, separated cleanly from what one might argue they could become.

AI for fundamental research — what's real, and what's still open

Cited context. The documented gains are real. DeepMind's AlphaFold reorganised structural biology, with AlphaFold 3 (2024) extending to biomolecular interactions, and autonomous "self-driving" laboratories — closed loops of robotics, machine learning and experiment — are reportedly accelerating materials and chemistry discovery several-fold . But the honest counter-evidence is documented too: a 2025 Nature analysis finds AI can boost individual scientists while narrowing collective exploration toward data-rich, computationally convenient problems, with automation bias lending false confidence that the whole territory is being searched — even as open-code/open-data practices have improved reproducibility .

FORWARD VIEWAI amplifies §05's pattern; it does not erase it. Today AI accelerates research most where the work is search, optimisation and scale-up — vast molecule, material and parameter spaces — and least on the conceptual gates. The hardest QC questions in §02 are conceptual: whether a topological qubit exists, or whether optical loss can beat threshold, are not obviously problems a larger search solves. So the likeliest near-term effect is to widen the §05 split — turbo-charging the scale-up game while the basic-science gates stay stubborn — rather than to dissolve it. Forecast, not fact; ledger id FW5.

Robotics + AI for industry — a different kind of S-curve

Cited context. Here the documented picture is lopsided: China installed roughly 295,000 industrial robots in 2024 — about 54% of the world's total — against roughly 34,200 in the US; its operational robot stock (~2.0M) is around five times the US (~0.39M), and its robot density has overtaken the US outright . China leads conventional factory automation, not just labour-cost manufacturing.

FORWARD VIEWLeapfrog the labour-cost game rather than re-fight it. §06's reshoring math is unforgiving because it competes on the old axis — labour and input cost — where the US starts 30–50% behind. The argued alternative is to compete on a new axis: convert the US lead in frontier AI and its deep capital markets into an automation-native industrial flywheel, where the unit of advantage is software, autonomy and capital rather than cheap labour. Trade-offs, stated plainly: China also leads robotics deployment (above), so this is not an empty field; it is capital-intensive and unproven at national scale; and it carries a real political economy of automation versus jobs. An option with costs, not a sure thing. Forecast/argument; ledger id FW6.

New tracks, not just more funding for old structures

Cited context. The "new tracks" idea is not hypothetical. Focused Research Organizations (Convergent Research; roughly ten times a typical grant, ~$55M, mission-scoped) attack tooling and dataset bottlenecks that universities under-serve; ARPA-style agencies (DARPA, ARPA-E, ARPA-H, the UK's ARIA) and NSF's National AI Research Institutes pursue high-risk, mission-driven work outside the standard grant . These exist and are growing — but they are young and small beside the university system.

FORWARD VIEWAdd new tracks; don't only widen the old. The argued thesis is a portfolio: keep the stable core university funding §06 calls for, and deliberately scale AI-native labs, FRO/ARPA-style mission organisations, and new funding and organisational models built around autonomous experimentation. The point is not to replace the old structures — most science still runs through universities — but to stop treating them as the only track. Honesty: these models are unproven at scale, and scaling them prematurely could starve the basic-science core. Argument; ledger id FW7.

Where humans sit — preparing concretely

The instinct to answer this with inspiration should be resisted; here is the concrete version. Autonomous labs still need humans to pose hypotheses, choose what is worth asking, and interpret what comes back — the bottleneck analyses are blunt that the binding constraints on progress are cultural and institutional, not compute . That points at specifics rather than slogans: design institutions for human-in-the-loop science; treat reproducibility infrastructure (shared code, data, instruments) as core capacity; and build the skills that compound with automation rather than compete with it — problem-framing, cross-domain synthesis, and the ability to build, direct and audit AI systems.

FORWARD VIEWBet on the complements, not the substitutes. For institutions: pilot AI-native and FRO/ARPA structures now, at small scale, and measure them honestly. For individuals: the durable position is upstream of the automatable step — asking the right question and judging the answer — not the step itself. This is prescription, not measurement; treat it as argument. Ledger id FW8.

Counter-view — or is this just the next round of hype?

The whole section could be wrong in a familiar way. Economic history has a productivity paradox — transformative technologies that showed up "everywhere but the productivity statistics" for years — and AI and automation have over-promised before, through more than one winter. Betting national strategy on a revolution that may arrive slower, or narrower, than its advocates claim is itself a risk, and the parts of research and industry that resist automation are often exactly the tacit-knowledge, human-institutional parts that techno-optimism discounts. A sober reading is that AI and robotics are real and important but not yet a license to rebuild everything around them.

COUNTER-VIEWDon't bet the strategy on a revolution that may underdeliver. The disciplined position is to pursue the new tracks as options, sized to what AI and robotics have actually delivered, while keeping the proven institutions funded — so that if the revolution arrives slower or narrower than promised, the foundation still stands. Weigh this against the forward views above rather than choosing once. Ledger id FW9.

The future rarely loses to the past by being outspent — it loses by being out-imagined. The honest task is to fund what works, build what might, and keep the two clearly labelled — which is, in the end, what this whole dossier has tried to do.
